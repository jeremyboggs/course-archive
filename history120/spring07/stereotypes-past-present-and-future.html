Stereotypes: Past, Present, and Future

A stereotype is a conception or opinion of people made from observations concerning people from a “group”. Stereotypes can be a positive reflection on a group of people or it can be a nasty reflection. For example, all blondes are stupid, or more seriously, all Jewish people are cheap (negative) or all Asians are smart (positive). Stereotypes are wrong either way since you are saying that every single member of that group is something without knowing any facts. Stereotypes are based on previous experience and then continued on in future experiences with someone different but from that same group. Stereotypes have been around for centuries. Americans did not want the Irish in their country, so instead of understanding them and giving them a chance, they judged them first and formed stereotypes against them. Americans also have seen woman for years as inferior to men, weak, and that they should be only objects that are seen and/or mothers. These and more stereotypes continue on today. New stereotypes of today include, all middle easterners are terrorists and Americans are fat. Nineteenth Century stereotypes do the same work as stereotypes today; both categorize and both wrongly judge people. It is not good that stereotypes are still carried on and that new ones are constructed everyday.

In the 1850’s the Irish started trickling over to America to escape poverty and famine, but they were directly unwanted. This can be explained in that Americans were afraid of change and afraid that these illiterate, poor, unskilled people would deteriorate their beloved country that they had recently created. Americans decided to view the Irish as mean, feisty, unruly, and “incapable of becoming American”. They were not given a chance to mingle in society, thus the Irish could not even try to become Americanized. The Irish were viewed the same way the North viewed the blacks. This stereotype is still accepted today, but a few things have changed. The Irish are still viewed as feisty and rough, but in a more positive way. The “fighting Irish” is a term used to describe them. This cartoon of the fighting Irish is associated of course with sports. But it depicts the fighting Irish in a good way. Meaning they are tough and will win against the other team. These days being tough and mean are ways of staying alive and surviving. The Irish must have stuck it out and survived since they are welcomed and loved these days. This stereotype changed over time, but not all do.

Women have always been seen as weak, pretty, or just a typical homemaker and mother. Basically one that has no influence on society and an object that should be concerned with appearance. Also, women are seen as those that gossip and talk about things that have no significance. This picture is from the 19th century. Women at that time were seen as inferior to men. They should not work, vote, or have any power. The men were the ones that had all control. Women those days thought their place was at home and taking care of the family because that’s what society was telling them by stereotyping them into this group. Today woman are encouraged to have careers but magazines mass advertise ways to look good in the work place. Both photos encourage an attractive appearance and both talk about petty insignificant “gossip”. The magazine clip give secret hints on where to find the best bargains or ways to look pretty in a short time. There is no encouragement to make an impact or columns informing them of important world topics. There are no “hints” on how to work your way to another level in society. Women are still being compressed into the circle of appearance and inferiority.

Stereotypes will forever be present. They may change over time but essentially they still do the same thing, categorize and wrongfully judge. Not all women are concerned with beauty and bargains. Some women work hard and are concerned with politics and ways to improve the world, but it is hard to find help when they are still being seen as pretty objects. I am sure not all Irish men drink and fight regularly. Continuing stereotypes just paves the way for new ones. A new stereotype today is that Americans are fat and infatuated with food, like in this photo. The clip shows a very jolly man, obviously an American, extremely happy to have so much unhealthy food. He does not seem to have another care in the world, he has what he wants. I chose to add this photo because it shows that new stereotypes are created frequently based on the fact that a large number of Americans are overweight. However this leads to an outsider viewing all Americans as overweight. Stereotypes do nothing to fix a problem. Instead they poke fun at the subject and force people to continue to view things the way they are. Stereotypes contribute nothing to society.
