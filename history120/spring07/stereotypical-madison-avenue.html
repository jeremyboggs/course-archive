Stereotypical Madison Avenue

Today in the United States certain stereotypes are used arguably for less nefarious reasons than they were in the nineteenth century. That is not to say, however, that modern day stereotypes are any more positive or constructive than those of our past. The stereotype of the lazy, drunken and brawling Irish “goon” who looked more like an animal is not significantly more positive to the Irish reputation than the more recent stereotype of the Irish party animal. The difference is that the purposes behind these respective uses of stereotyping are almost complete opposites. Generally speaking, the “cultural work” performed by stereotypes in the nineteenth century was to assist in efforts to exclude and marginalize particular groups who sought acceptance within the American experience, while the “cultural work” performed by stereotypes today seems more akin to “marketing support” work, aimed at attaching some well known collective attribute to various consumer products for all to enjoy. While the caricature of the Irish goon was designed and used to keep the Irish outside the bounds of decent American society, the “party Mick” is now constantly used to lure others into the happy drinking ways of this lovable member of the big American quilt.

While it would be naive to suggest that there are no longer efforts to exclude and marginalize various groups within the US, it is clear that America is longer a place where overt racism can be practiced in the media to the extent it once was in the nineteenth century. The level of social consciousness and moral restraints on overt racism has increased significantly from days ago, and the fact is many ethnic groups that were once on the outside looking in have now worked their way into the current American mainstream. Thus, while attempts to negatively influence the American public’s acceptance of a particular group by way of a blatant stereotype broadcast widely in the mainstream media may have been tolerated in the nineteenth century, they would most likely be regarded as heavy-handed and morally unacceptable in today’s America. In other words, racism and the use of stereotypes in support of racism, must be much more subtle and limited in audience scope today.

On the other hand, the use of stereotypes are powerful ways to communicate a message. Never willing to pass up a way to sell a message, thus, Madison Avenue has made itself all the more effective by playing on the same or similar stereotypes as have persisted through the years - - just with a different twist. This is precisely why, for example, the alcoholic Irishman stereotype has managed to survive. Instead of being portrayed as the feckless, drunken rascal who would bring nothing but crime and disorderly conduct to America, the alcoholic Irishman stereotype has now been watered down a wee bit to represent the new carefree, hard drinking, hard-playing man among men who we should all join and emulate at the nearest bar selling Coors Light. Irish Americans are still being cast as drunks, but they are now acceptable and even lovable – so much so, indeed, that we are all urged to be just like them!

The images I selected to post strongly support the idea that, while stereotypes may stay the same through the years in terms of basic substance, they can change dramatically in terms of their uses and intended purposes. One of the nineteenth century cartoons I posted and the modern day cartoon I posted both address the alcoholic Irishman stereotype. That, however, is where the similarity stops. Clearly, one cartoon intends to use the stereotype to the detriment of the Irish, whereas the recent cartoon seeks to convey a much more positive view of an Irishman with drink in hand. The stereotypical Irish alcoholic who was once an unacceptable threat to decent Americans has apparently been transformed into a vital component of the leisure life of modern America, all through the magic of TV commercials.
