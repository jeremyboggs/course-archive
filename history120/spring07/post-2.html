post #2

Images can be very useful for historians to determine one side’s feelings towards an event in history. For example, this picture, by Paul Revere, of the Boston Massacre portrays the American’s view of what happened there. The British commander is standing behind his troops giving them the orders almost as if he is unwilling to do any of the dirty work himself. The British troops are in front of a building with a sign that reads “Butchers House” on it suggesting that they are “butchering” the Americans. The American’s in this picture out number the British about 2-1. However the Americans are not fighting back against the British. Also the fight is happening in the middle of a street, in front of a church, at night. This helps to portray the British as evil since its in front of a church, and at night.

Paul Revere uses this picture to depict the British as evil, and probably to help gain support from the colonists for the revolution. The British are firing on people that are completely defenseless, that show no signs of instigation, and who are not fighting back. Revere even changes the name of the building they are in front of from custom house to Butchers house. The leader of the British troops is standing there calling out orders to his men, encouraging them to continue to add to the violence. All of this adds to the idea that Revere wanted this to appear as brutal, and as uncalled for as possible. Pictures such as this suggest that revolutionary leaders were very creative in their attempts to gain support for their cause. It also serves to illustrate one potential view of this event. The reason that there may be several different accounts of an event like this is because people tend to exaggerate stories to make actions resulting from it seem more justified. Phenomena like this can help historians to begin to realize how stories can spread and create more support for actions, and how historical events can be altered to show one side more negatively then the other.
