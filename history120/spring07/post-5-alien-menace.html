Post #5: Alien Menace

Every race has a few stereotypes that follow them where they go.  From Asian people being good at math to Jewish people being very greedy with money, stereotypes have no mercy.  It is very difficult not to offend anyone since there are so many people, keeping racism a touchy subject.  Even in class, when we talk about race or a group of people, we know what the answers are or what the professor is looking for, but since that race is represented in the class, we do not want to offend them and simply try not to be called on.  Stereotypes label races a certain way and that certain way is how the majority of the population see them as.

A few things have changed, since the 19th century, but there still remain stereotypes that continue to label races in order to simplify things.  I found a few pictures in the Library of Congress archives that depict typical African-American and Chinese-Americans in the early 19th century.  In the picture with the small child enjoying his watermelon, you see a large chunk of it already eaten.  As you know, African-Americans like watermelon, chicken, and grits, this has been a stereotype for a long time and is even the title to the Nappy Roots album, “Watermelon, Chicken, & Gritz” with a “z” because it makes it that much cooler.  The little boy, named “Watermelon Jake”, is not wearing any shoes and appears to be sitting on a table.  So at this time period, African-Americans are poor people who cannot afford shoes and enjoy watermelon in the summer time to keep them cool.

In the other photo, entitled “The Chinese Must Go!” there are several small pictures showing several stereotypes of Chinese people.  In one picture, there is a line of people waiting to hand there clothes to the Chinese cleaner man who is already hard at work.  In another picture, there is a man inspecting the clothes that a Chinese tailor has finished for him.  In yet another picture, there is a man looking to buy cigars in a Chinese owned Cigar shop.  These are stereotypes of jobs that the Chinese had and in every single picture, they are still wearing their typical Chinese clothing, while the white people have regular trousers, coats, hats, and shoes.

Some of these stereotypes are still valid today.  Of course some of them are not also.  In a photo that I found in the Google search engine, there is a picture of Tupac.  This “Gangsta” rapper emerged late in the 20th century to become one of the biggest rap icons in history.  This photo also depicts what a typical gangster is, I guess, supposed to look like.  From a huge tattoo across his stomach stating, “THUG LIFE” with a bullet as the letter “i”, to his jeans slung low passed his boxer briefs, to his backwards bandana.  Yet he finds it necessary to wear a crucifix outside of it all.  This “thug” was later gunned down in 1996 in a drive by with his murder yet to be solved.  So when Tupac became rich and famous, he wore better clothes, had more jewelry and was very influential in the music world, but little Watermelon Jake was just a little kid with no shoes enjoying his juicy watermelon.

In the last photo, I found what appears to be a web advertisement for a Chinese tailor.  There are dressed in there traditional clothing and the older man is working on a piece of clothing in the background.  The bottom of the picture states “We don’t have a fancy website, but we make the most beautiful dresses.”  It also says that they are made by hand.  This made me think, “Did they wear those clothes on purpose to attract customers?”  You want to make money when you start a business, but I do not think that you have to wear something or look a certain way that you would not normally look in order to attract customers, your work should speak for itself.

Stereotyping is a serious problem that people have dealt with for a very long time.  Some of these stereotypes, although there is some truth, hold back the race from breaking free of the mold that society have put them in.  There are a few pioneers, such as Dave Chappelle, Carlos Mencia, and the creators of South Park that have confronted stereotypes and made them public so that everyone knows what it does.  Now I’m going to get out of my house that has like 20 people in it, drive to school in my Toyota Corolla with Wal-Mart spinners, and stop by Taco Bell before I get to class.
