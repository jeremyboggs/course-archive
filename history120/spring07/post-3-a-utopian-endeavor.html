Post #3: A Utopian Endeavor

The Jacksonian era was a time of major upheaval, from the lives of ordinary citizens to the government and its policies, so a utopian society would have to account for these changes. Politically, the Nullification Crisis caused quite a ripple as the power of States to disagree with the Federal Government came into question. Socially, temperance and suffrage were in vogue. In terms of religion, the Jacksonian era came on the heels of the Second Great Awakening. Economically, the North and South were split into Industrial and Agricultural production respectively; also Jackson’s move to take power away from the Federal Bank resulted in some inflation and distrust of citizens in paper money. Culturally, The role of the individual was stressed and given agency, as opposed to the days where only those of wealth and property would have a voice. The utopian community I put forth takes the above into account. A few key assumptions/purposeful decisions about the community are: that the community is intended to exist in South Carolina and be representative of their views.

The community is governed by a council elected by the people (giving the common man more power), and a chairman to preside is elected by the council from their number. Their responsibilities pick up where Federal, State and local governing bodies left off. First, they oversee the economic wellbeing of the community. The council takes on leadership more in a business sense than a political one. The council is also responsible for proposing laws, though the decision to enact them would be by vote of the community. The laws’ general purpose in the community is as counterparts to the religious aspect, to prevent radicalism or abuse of the community and its ideals. The inhabitants’ vote in State or Federal elections is a matter of acting in the interest of the community, so a liaison from the council would be responsible for representing them to the State legislature (and thus betting against the Federal government in a power struggle).

Looking to the map of the community reveals the redress of many of the problems and issues of the Jacksonian era. The center of the community is the church (citing the recent revival), which serves as a social hub to cement the relationships of those in the community. The same building would also host the governing body. Presumably, at least some of the land used for the construction of the utopia would be in use as farmland. This would remain, and be run as a business by the community council. A few factories would be constructed as a “hedge bet” against an uncertain financial future (citing the recent tariff fiasco). The government would run both institutions: hiring employees, building more factories if that proves to be the more profitable route and keeping with the times progressively. As paper money was trusted less (citing the fallout of Jackson’s attack on the Bank), employees would be offered a part of their salary in goods at a government owned store where the markup would be a certain percentage set by law to create one source of revenue for the government. The businesses would be jointly owned by all the citizens. There is no place within the community where one might purchase drink (citing the temperance movement). The factories and farms are placed in proximity to prevent a rift from forming between the employees of each, and they are placed so that there is room for growth. As much as possible, the utopia would be self-sufficient.
