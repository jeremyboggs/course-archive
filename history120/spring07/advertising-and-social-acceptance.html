Advertising and Social Acceptance

Advertising has been a source of media that can be used to see the social trends of different times by seeing what is being portrayed in advertising.Throughout advertising history, a focus has been given to selling products with a promise of social acceptance, because not being accepted socially is a major individual fear for most people.
One advertisement that uses social acceptance as a selling point is this Barbasol ad. This advertisement is selling Barbasol shaving cream, and depicts a bride-to-be talking with her mother about the man she is about to marry. It is implied that the mother is complaining that the man is not rich. The woman’s reply to this, which is printed is ” but he doesn’t have to be rich, mother. He’s got a Barbasol Face!” This sends out the message to men that being poor will hinder them from finding a wife, unless of course they use Barbasol shaving cream. The advertisement plays on the insecurities men have about not being rich, and shows that with Barbasol that they too can get a beautiful wife as easily as a rich man. It is interesting to note that the image depicted is of the mother altering the wedding dress for the bride. That practice was a lot more common in those days than it is now, and reflects back on the time in which it was made. Also, it is interesting that the advertisement focuses on marraige, because in mainstream culture in those days portraying sex was still taboo. An example of this is in television of that time where couples slept in different beds.

Fast-forwarding to today, another hygiene advertisement is this Axe ad. This advertisement is for Axe body spray, and depicted in the image is a cracked open apartment door with a ticket machine attached that says “Take a number” as would be found in a grocery store. This ad is insinuating that this guy who owns the apartment uses axe body spray, and is flocked by so many girls because of this, that he had to put a ticket machine on his door. Further insinuation is that the door is cracked open, because the girls come and go so rapidly that he doesn’t even have time to close the door. This is selling the social acceptance of the female sex towards the male who wears Axe body spray. It is saying that if a man wears Axe, he will be flocked by women wanting to have sex with him. This shows an interesting contrast with the Barbasol advertisement. In the more prude times the Barbasol ad uses marraige, and in the the more sexually overdosed present, the promise of multiple sexual partners is presented.

A third advertisement that uses social acceptance as a selling point is this advertisement for Courtley Toiletries. The ad shows the Courtley Toiletries set in a very regal setting, characterized by the dark red and gold colors. Also displayed prominently are two gold-outlined horses. Owning horses throughout time has signified wealth and prominence, because they are an expensive endeavor. In addition, the text shown in the largest font on the advertisement states, “First choice of men who know.” This makes it seem like there is a high-class backing of this product, and any man who chooses to buy this product will be considered high-class. This advertisement promises the social acceptance of the high class, which many men dream of one day becoming a part of.

The fourth ad that uses social acceptance as a selling point is this Michelob Beer ad. This ad is selling Michelob Beer with the tagline “Lose the carbs. Not the taste.” Also depicted in the background, is a physically fit man doing sit-ups. This ad insinuates that if you choose to drink Michelob Beer, you will become physically fit, while you will gain pounds by drinking other beers. In the society of today, there is an “obesity epidemic”, and also thanks to modern popular media, there is a great social importance on being healthy, fit, and not fat. Those of larger structure have a greater tendency to be insecure about their weight, and being accepted into modern society with that weight. This advertisement plays into those insecurities by promising that by drinking their beer one can get fit, and be wholly accepted by society.

Whether being accepted by a future wife, the opposite sex, a group of people, or society as a whole, social acceptance is a grand part of human existence. Throughout many different changes within the different idealogies of different times, the fear of not being accepted is a constant.
