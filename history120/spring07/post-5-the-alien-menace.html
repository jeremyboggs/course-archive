Post #5: The Alien Menace

By definition, a stereotype is a simplified and standardized conception or image invested with special meaning and held in common by members of a group [1]. They can be harmful or beneficial, correct or incorrect, and have been used for many different reasons. Furthermore, stereotypes are always in flux as they are modified to conform to changes in society over time. Yet, from nineteenth century to present day America, stereotypes have always performed a certain type of “cultural work”, in that they have always been used to define and protect the individuality and uniqueness of any given culture.

In the nineteenth century, the United States experienced a major surge of immigration from foreign cultures that were markedly different than the existing American culture. These immigrants, who were abundant in Irish-Catholics and Chinese, posed a threat by compromising what natives had previously developed as the “American” culture. As a response, immigrants were often mocked and stereotyped in cartoons of newspapers in order to distinguish American traits from alien traits. An example of this can be seen in this 1880 cartoon from the magazine, Harper’s Weekly, where a Chinese man is depicted coming to America in a tea pot while saying “How e do Melican man. Me comme plenty time. Bling big heap Chineeman”. By using the teapot as a symbol for Chinese culture, this cartoon implies that the Chinese are making America their own home, and not conforming to traditional American values. By pointing out and making fun of the Chinese man’s poor grammar, and exaggerating unfamiliar stereotypical physical features such as clothing, facial structure, and hairstyle, the author helps define what is not American. Thus, in return, stereotypes such as these protect what is American by not allowing alien cultures to blend in.

As mentioned earlier, the Chinese were not the only threat to American culture. In fact, prior to the mass immigration of Chinese, Irish-Catholics were the major threat to the existing American culture, which was mainly Protestant. Protestant Americans were afraid the growing number of Catholics would become too influential, and possibly seize control of the government. In magazine cartoons such as this, which was also found in Harper’s Weekly, the stereotype that the Irish were ape-like barbarians that were prone to drunkenness and lawlessness was reinforced time and time again. Depicting them in this animalistic manner made all Irish seem untrustworthy, and fueled anti-Catholic notions. This widespread negative preconceived notion about the Irish kept them in check, and helped minimize their influence among American society. Also, by making fun of the drunken, disorderly behavior of the Irish, Americans at the same time were defining themselves as level headed, in control people.

In present day times, America has become a melting pot society. It has become more and more homogenous, and people of different cultures are beginning to lose their discrete identities. As a result, overtly racist stereotypes in newspapers and media (such as the examples above) marginalized significantly in the twentieth century. Yet, stereotypes still do exist today, and are still used to protect traditional cultural values. For example, television commercials often portray the stereotype that drug users are losers, which is a mechanism for promoting a sober, healthy lifestyle to future generations. Similarly, this image depicts how Uncle Sam teaches us drugs are bad. Cultural values must be taught to future generations for them to be preserved. Also, because of the melting pot of cultures in today’s America, and the gradual loss of discrete identities among cultures, people sometimes manifest stereotypes given to the group of people they feel most a part of. For instance, if a Caucasian boy was raised in an African-American community, he or she might wear baggy clothes in hopes to fit in.

As one can see, times have radically changed since nineteenth century America, but stereotypes have always been used to define and protect the individuality and uniqueness of a group’s culture. Throughout history stereotypes have proven to be the cause of many problems and misconceptions, but fortunately, overtly racist ones are becoming less common. But in the end, as long as there are cultural differences, there will also be stereotypes to draw between them.
